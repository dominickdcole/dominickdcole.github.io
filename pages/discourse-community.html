What this piece is

This is a discourse-community profile of aerospace tooling and manufacturing engineers — the people who design the jigs and fixtures that make aircraft parts buildable. It's grounded in a primary interview with an experienced tooling engineer, and it maps how that community communicates: its genres, its vocabulary, and the way trust gets built less through certifications than through reputation.

"A young engineer with a degree but no program experience will be respected, but they won't be trusted yet."

The genre, and why it's here

A field profile sits between journalism and analysis. Unlike the personal statement, the writer steps back — the subject is someone else's world, and the job is to observe it accurately and explain it to outsiders. It draws on primary research and direct quotation, then organizes that raw material into claims a reader can follow.

The challenge here was synthesis: turning a wide-ranging conversation into a structured account of how information flows across a full program, from a customer's statement of work down to a delivered tool. I had to decide which details carried weight, how to frame the same technical idea for different audiences, and where a direct quote said it better than I could.

What it shows about my range

Against the other pieces, this one demonstrates analytical and reportorial writing — handling sourced material responsibly, integrating quotation, and shaping observation into a coherent argument about how a professional culture actually works.
