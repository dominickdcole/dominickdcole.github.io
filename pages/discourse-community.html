What this piece is

This is a discourse community profile of aerospace tooling and manufacturing engineers. These are the people who design the jigs and fixtures that make aircraft parts buildable. The piece is based on an interview with an experienced tooling engineer, and it maps how that community communicates. It looks at their genres, their vocabulary, and the way trust gets built. In this field, trust comes less from certifications and more from reputation.

"A young engineer with a degree but no program experience will be respected, but they won't be trusted yet."

The genre, and why it's here

A field profile sits between journalism and analysis. Unlike the personal statement, the writer steps back. The subject is someone else's world, and the job is to observe it accurately and explain it to outsiders. It relies on primary research and direct quotation, then organizes that raw material into claims a reader can follow.

The challenge here was synthesis. I had to turn a wide-ranging conversation into a clear account of how information moves across a full program, from a customer's statement of work down to a delivered tool. That meant deciding which details mattered, framing the same technical idea for different readers, and knowing when a direct quote said it better than I could.

What it shows about my range

Set against the other pieces, this one shows analytical and reporting skills. It handles sourced material responsibly, works direct quotation into the text, and shapes observation into a clear argument about how a professional culture actually works.
